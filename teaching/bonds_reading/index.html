Introduction

We discuss bonds to introduce the accounting for long-term liabilities. Bonds are a typical example of these.

A bond is a promissory note issued in the financial markets, and is usually tradable by its holders (the retail lenders) in the markets.

We will focus on a particular type of bond called a coupon bond.

A coupon bond promises to pay a lumpsum amount at its maturity date, as well as regular fixed payments called coupons during the bond life.

Key concepts

Face value

The face value of a bond is the lumpsum amount paid at the bond maturity date. We can think of it as similar to returning the amount borrowed via the bond issue, or the principal.

Coupon

The coupon is the fixed regular payment during the bond life (issue date to maturity date). We can think of this as similar to a regular interest payment on the amount borrowed via the bond issue. A common pattern is for the coupon payments to be semi-annual or twice a year.

Coupon rate

The coupon rate is the annual coupon divided by the face value. Conventionally, the coupon rate is stated as part of the bond description, for example "A company issues a 2 percent bond" means that the bond has a coupon rate of 2 percent.

Proceeds

The proceeds are the amount collected from the bond markets when the bond is issued, or the amount borrowed. This need not equal the face value (the amount paid back at maturity).

If the prevailing market interest rates when the bond is issued are more than the bond coupon rate, then the markets will pay less for the bond than the face value. The proceeds in this case will equal the present value of the bond payments discounted at the market interest rate, so that the bond investors will effectively get the market interest rate from their investment. In this case, we say that the bond is issued at a discount.

Yield to maturity (YTM)

The yield to maturity (YTM) is the interest rate that a bond buyer gets when they buy the bond at the current market price and hold it to maturity.

Equivalently, the yield to maturity is the discount rate that makes the present value of the remaining bond cashflows equal to the current market price of the bond.

Effective interest rate (EIR)

The effective interest rate (EIR) is the YTM at the bond issue date based on setting the proceeds net of issue costs equal to the present value of the bond cash flows.

The effective interest rate is used to compute the bond-related interest expense.

Though for calculating interest, we use the EIR based on any convenient convention such as semi-annual compounding, when reporting the EIR in the notes, we state it as an annually compounded rate.

Suppose that we are given a semiannually compounded rate, say rs; and we want to find the equivalent annually compounded rate, say ra.

We set the future value of $1 after 1 year computed using the semiannually compounded rate, equal to the future value of $1 after 1 year computed using the annually compounded rate. In other words, we solve the following equation for ra.

( 1 + r s 2 ) 2 = 1 + r a .

We can use a similar approach to find the annually compounded rate given a rate based on any other compounding frequency, say quarterly. We solve for the future value after 1 year, and use that to find the annually compounded rate.

Interest schedule

A loan amortization schedule or interest schedule is a useful worksheet in bond accounting. We can refer to it to find the interest expense for each period.

The interest expense for a period is calculated using the EIR, not the coupon rate. Therefore, it need not match the coupon payment for the period.

If the interest expense is less than the coupon payment, then the extra amount of coupon payment is treated as early repayment of the bond obligation (reducing the liability).

If the interest expense is more than the coupon payment, then the extra amount of interest expense is treated as adding on to the bond obligation (increasing the liability).

The following is an example of the format of a loan amortization schedule. (The interest calculation shown is based on a semi-annually compounded EIR).

Period ending Beg. liability [1] Less: Payment [2] Interest basis [3] (= [1] - [2]) Interest expense [4] (= EIR × 0.5 × [3]) End. liability [5] (=[3]+[4])
