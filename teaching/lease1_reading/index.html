An economist would argue that the example above is unrealistic. If markets are functioning properly, the present value of renting out an asset for its entire life should always be the same as that of selling it.

Accountants adopt this economist logic by treating such lease transactions as though they were an outright sale of the asset financed by a receivable equal to the present value of the lease payments.

This asserts the accounting principle of substance over form. In accounting for a lease transaction, what matters is the economic reality of the transaction (substance), not so much how it is legally structured (form).

Substance over form

In some circumstances, a transaction that is legally a lease may in economic terms more closely resemble a purchase of the leased asset funded by a stream of payments that resemble those from a mortgage loan. For example, the lease term could be close to the entire life of the asset, so that the lessee (borrower) effectively controls the asset through most of its life. Another situation could be that the present value of the lease payments are approximately equal to the fair value of the leased asset at lease commencement, so that the lessee is effectively paying about as much for the lease as they would to buy the asset (which a rational party would not pay unless the value received from the use of the asset is equivalent to that from buying the asset.)

In such circumstances, we assert a substance over form criterion; and account for the lease as though the asset had been sold to the lessee in exchange for a receivable.

Accounting for lessors

In a lease (rental) agreement, the lessor is the supplier of the leased (rented) asset; while the lessee is the borrower of the leased asset. In this reading, we focus on the accounting for a lessor.

Types of lessor

Financier lessor

A financier lessor is a business whose business model is to buy an asset and rent it out; and who make money primarily from the spread between the periodic payments received from the lessee and the periodic mortgage payments they make to their supplier of finance. This business model is similar to that of a bank (financier) except that instead of lending cash, they are lending a fixed asset.

Example 2: Financier lessor

is in the aircraft leasing business. They buy an aircraft for $, using a mortgage loan with equal annual payments of $ per year in arrears for 10 years, based on an annual interest rate of percent. They lease the aircraft to an airline for 10 years at an annual rental of $ paid in arrears. The stream of rental receipts from the aircraft is similar to those from lending $ for 10 years via a mortgage loan with equal payments at the higher interest rate of percent. Like a bank, makes money via the spread between the effective interest rate they earn on their lending versus the effective interest rate they pay on their borrowing.

Manufacturer-dealer lessor

A manufacturer or dealer may offer a lease as an alternative to purchasing their product. The customer may prefer this option due to the payment pattern and avoiding the need to dispose of the used asset.

Example 3: Manufacturer

A computer manufacturer supplies desktop personal computers to a University. As an alternative to buying the computers outright, the manufacturer offers a three-year lease of the computers (approximately equal to their life).

Example 4: Dealer

A car dealer sells cars to retail customers. As an alternative to buying a car, the car dealer offers the option of leasing it for a fixed period.

In this reading, we will by default take the viewpoint of a financier lessor, and will discuss the differences in accounting for a manufacturer-dealer lessor in the last section.

Operating and Finance leases

Based on substance over form, we classify lease agreements for a lessor into:

  1. Transactions whose economic substance or business purpose does represent a temporary lending of the asset in order to earn a periodic rental payment. Such leases are called operating leases.
  2. Transactions whose economic substance or business purpose is similar to an outright sale of the asset in exchange for a stream of periodic payments (a receivable). Such leases are called finance leases.

Finance lease

A lease is treated as a finance lease if any one of the following conditions is satisfied:

  1. The title to the asset (ownership) automatically passes to the lessee during or at the end of the lease term.
  2. The lease contract gives the lessee the right to buy the asset at a bargain price during the lease term (bargain purchase option).
  3. The term of the lease is a major part of the asset useful life. As a thumbrule based on past US practice, we can use around 75% of the life as the lower limit of what is a major part.
  4. The present value of the lease payments, discounted at the implicit interest rate of the lease (defined later), equals substantially all of the fair value of the asset at the time the lease agreement is decided (inception date of the lease). As a thumbrule based on past US practice, we can use around 90% of the fair value as the lower limit of what is substantially all.
  5. The leased asset is so specialized that the only use that the lessor has for it is to lease it out to the particular lessee.

If any of these conditions is satisfied, the lease is in substance more like an outright sale of the asset than a rental arrangement.

Finance lease accounting

The inception date of the lease is the date when the lease was agreed upon or committed to.

The commencement date of the lease is the date from which the lease term actually starts.

The lessor treats a finance lease like a credit sale of the asset. At the commencement of the lease, the leased asset is credited as if sold and taken off the lessor's books. At the same time, a receivable is recognized in the amount of the present value of the lease payments that will be received in exchange.

Implicit interest rate

For a financier lessor, the implicit interest rate is the discount rate under which:

The present value (at that rate) of:

  • the lease payments (including any guaranteed amount of residual value, and
  • any ungaranteed residual value at lease-end;

EQUALS:

  • The fair value of the leased asset at lease inception, PLUS
  • any initial direct costs that the lessor incurs in order to establish the lease (lessor IDC).

The fair value of the asset plus the lessor IDC represents what the lessor has given up in the lease transaction.

The present value of the lease payments plus the present value of the unguaranteed residual value at lease-end, represent what the lessor gets in exchange.

These should be equal in a fair market transaction.

Lease payments typically include the periodic rental payments plus the guaranteed residual value at lease-end.

If the periodic payment is a mix of rental payments plus payments for something else such as reimbursement of executory costs, we should exclude amounts that are not directly for the rental.

Manufacturer-dealer lessor

The accounting for a manufacturer-dealer lessor differs from that for a financier lessor primarily in the initial recognition of a finance lease. The later accounting for the lease receivable is similar to that for a financier lessor.

Implicit interest rate

For a manufacturer-dealer lessor, we treat the finance lease similarly to a regular sale of goods. The lessor initial direct costs would be classified similarly to "selling costs" in this case, and consistent with the treatment of selling costs in a regular sales transaction, would be expensed, not capitalized. Consistent with this logic, the lessor IDC is not reflected as part of the implicit interest rate calculation for a manufacturer-dealer lessor. The equation becomes:

The present value (at the implicit interest rate) of:

  • the lease payments (including any guaranteed amount of residual value, and
  • any ungaranteed residual value at lease-end;

EQUALS:

  • The fair value of the leased asset at lease inception.

Market rate

Unlike a financier lessor, the manufacturer-dealer lessor does not primarily make money from the interest rate spread between the implicit interest rate and their borrowing rate. Rather, they make money from the profit on the sale, or the gap between the sale price and their production costs for the leased item.

The manufacturer-dealer lessor may therefore sometimes structure the finance lease transaction so that it reflects a lower-than-market promotional implicit interest rate on the lease, or even a zero or negative interest rate. In this case, it would be misleading to use the implicit interest rate to compute the present value of the lease payments.

The standard therefore stipulates that, in situations where the implicit interest rate deviates from the market interest rate, we should use the market interest rate instead to calculate the lease receivable. (Effectively, we can just use the market interest rate always.)

Sales and COGS

Sales revenue

Sales revenue is computed as the present value of the lease payments, discounted at the market interest rate. This is the same as the lease receivable less the present value of the unguaranteed residual value.

If the present value of the lease payments is greater than the fair value of the leased asset, then the sales revenue is capped at the fair value.

Cost of goods sold (COGS)

Cost of goods sold is computed using the asset carrying amount in inventory (applying the FIFO or weighted average convention as per the company policy) LESS the present value of the unguaranteed residual value (discounted at the market interest rate).

Effectively, the unguaranteed residual value portion of the asset is treated as a non-sale, just a temporary loan of the asset.
