Bargain purchase option

A bargain purchase option (BPO) is the right for the lessee to buy the leased asset during the lease term at a price that is clearly a bargain, that is, a rational person would buy the asset at that price.

If the lease contract contains a BPO, then we assume that the lessee will exercise it. Then the lease payments end with the bargain purchase.

The right-of-use asset in this case is depreciated over the asset life rather than the lease term. After the purchase occurs, it is retitled.

Sale-leasebacks

A sale-leaseback is a transaction where the owner of an asset sells it to a financier lessor and then immediately leases it back for some fixed period. The usual business purpose of this transaction is to generate cash from an illiquid asset while continuing to use it.

In substance, some sale-leaseback transactions may be viewed as similar to a secured loan. IFRS 16 (Singapore FRS(I) 16) requires us to assess whether the initial sale of the asset is a true sale under IFRS 15 (Singapore FRS(I) 15); a true sale meaning that control of the asset is truly transferred to the buyer at point of sale in substance rather than just in form. It the transaction is determined to be not a true sale, then it is accounted for just as a secured loan, not as a lease.

Example

Seller-Lessee Co has an asset with fair value CU (at sale-leaseback inception). The company purchased the asset years ago at an acquisition cost of CU , and the current carrying amount is CU .

Seller-Lessee Co sells the asset to Buyer-Lessor Co at a price of CU , and immediately leases it back for years, its remaining life, in exchange for annual lease payments of CU in advance.

The accountants for both parties (independently) determine that this is not a true sale under IFRS 15 (Singapore FRS(I) 15). As such, the asset is accounted for as before, and the transaction is treated as a loan with Seller-Lessee borrowing CU and repaying it in instalments of CU per year in advance.

Henceforth, we will only consider the situation where the initial sale is viewed as a true sale. In this case the following rules apply.

  1. The initial sale is always recorded as being for sales proceeds equal to the fair value of the asset.
    1. If the sale proceeds are less than the fair value, we account for this by treating the difference as an imputed advance lease payment on the leaseback. That is, we account for the sale as if the buyer-lessor paid the full fair value, and then the seller-lessee immediately gave back some amount as an advance payment on the leaseback.
    2. If the sale proceeds are greater than the fair value, we account for this by treating the extra amount as a separate loan from the buyer-lessor to the seller-lessee.
  2. The right-of-use (ROU) asset is measured as follows at the start of the leaseback. If CA is the carrying amount of the asset on the seller-lessee's books just prior to the sale, FV is the fair value of the asset at sale-leaseback inception, and LL is the initial lease liability (before any advance payments), then the ROU asset is initially measured at: CA × LL FV
    3. Subsequently it is depreciated over the leaseback term.

Sale-leaseback with sale at more than fair value

In this case, we compute the loan liability as the portion of the sales price that is above the fair value.

Then the lease liability (before advance payments) is given by the difference between the present value of the leaseback payments and the loan liability amount.

We also have to split the periodic payments under the leaseback into payments for the lease and payments for the loan. We do this in proportion to the amounts of the initial lease liability (before advance payments) and loan liability.
