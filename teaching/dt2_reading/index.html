Disclaimer

In this topic, we study how to do the financial reporting for a reporting entity's income tax obligations. We do not learn the tax rules here, that is left to other courses. Our examples and exercises refer to some assumed tax rules that serve to illustrate the particular accounting issue at hand. These given rules do not necessarily reflect the actual tax rules.

Asset Tax Basis: constructive calculation

Paragraph 7 of SFRS(I) 1-12 provide a definition of the tax basis of an asset that we can use to establish a formula to calculate the tax basis.

Let us define the terms that we will use to build the formula.

Carrying amount (CA)

The carrying amount of the asset is the amount at which it is carried on the balance sheet, i.e., its net book value for financial reporting purposes.

Future taxable amount (FTA)

An asset by definition is held to generate future economic benefits. When the asset carrying amount is recovered via future economic benefits, how much of this recovery will be subject to tax? This is the future taxable amount (FTA). Recovery is via the usual business purpose for that asset (e.g., by using the asset for PPE, or selling it for an asset held for sale).

The FTA measures how much future taxable income is stored in the accounting carrying amount of the asset (which will lead to future taxes unless offset by future deductions).

Future deductible amount (FDA)

The company may be eligible to claim future deductions to offset against the future taxable income represented by the FTA. The amount of such future deductions is the future deductible amounts (FDA).

Deriving the TB formula

The taxable temporary differences (TTD) or deductible temporary differences (DTD) can directly be defined in terms of the FTA and FDA.

FTA > FDA

If FTA > FDA, then we have taxable temporary differences given by: [Equation (1)]

TTD = FTA - FDA .

This follows directly from the meaning of taxable temporary differences: the future amounts that will be subject to tax net of related tax deductions.

Recall that we earlier (Deferred Tax reading 1) defined TTD as follows: [Equation(2)]

TTD = CA - TB .

From Equations (1) and (2), we have:

TB = CA - FTA + FDA .

Noting that this is the same as TB=CA-TTD, we can see that the tax basis is the portion of the carrying amount that does not lead to net future taxable amounts.

FTA < FDA

If FTA < FDA, then we have deductible temporary differences given by: [Equation (1)]

DTD = FDA - FTA .

This follows directly from the meaning of deductible temporary differences: the net future tax deductions arising from the asset that will lead to tax savings.

Recall that we earlier (Deferred Tax reading 1) defined DTD for an asset as follows: [Equation(2)]

DTD = TB - CA .

From Equations (1) and (2), we have:

TB = CA - FTA + FDA .

Noting that this is the same as TB=CA+DTD, we can see that the tax basis is the carrying amount plus the net future deductions arising from it.

Paragraph 7

From the analysis above, we have a mechanical formula to compute the tax basis of an asset, and do not have to imagine a tax-rules-based balance-sheet.

Here is the formula again:

TB = CA - FTA + FDA .

The definition of tax basis in SFRS(I) 1-12:7 is directly in terms of this formula. Here is the quote.

" The tax base of an asset is the amount that will be deductible for tax purposes against any taxable economic benefits that will flow to an entity when it recovers the carrying amount of the asset. If those economic benefits will not be taxable, the tax base of the asset is equal to its carrying amount. "

Liability tax basis

SFRS(I)1-12:8 [paragraph 8] provides the definition for the tax basis of a liability. The definition distinguishes between two types of liability: unearned revenue, and other general liabilities. Let us first consider the case of general liabilities other than unearned revenue.

General liability

In the case of a liability, the carrying amount represents a stored up future loss rather than future income. The question then is, whether this loss, when it happens, will be tax-deductible. If it is, then the amount of the deduction is future deductible amount (FDA).

The deductible temporary differences are simply equal to these future deductible amounts. We have: (Equation 1-1)

DTD = FDA .

But we also have (from deferred tax reading 1) [Equation 1-2]

DTD = CA − TB .

Equating the right hand sides of Equations 1-1 and 1-2, we have:

CA − TB = FDA .

which implies:

TB = CA − FDA .

In some exotic situations, taxable income may also arise at the same time as the loss (FTA). For example, a foreign currency liability may have a taxable foreign currency gain at the same time as its outflow occurs. We can extend our formula to accommodate such situations:

TB = CA − FDA [ + FTA ] .

The tax basis is the carrying amount less the net future deductible amounts. Net future deductible amounts are future deductible amounts less any future taxable amounts arising together with them.

Unearned revenue

In the case of unearned revenue, future taxable income may arise when the related revenue is earned (the unearned revenue commitment is fulfilled).

Alternatively, depending on the tax rules, the unearned revenue amount may already have been taxed, for example at the point when it was received.

From these alternatives, SFRS(I) 1-12:8 (paragraph 8) gives us the following prescription to find the tax basis of unearned revenue.

TB = CA − future non-taxable amounts.

That is, the tax basis (TB) is the carrying amount (CA) less the portion that has already been taxed in the past.

The future non-taxable amount provides the basis for the future tax savings arising from the liability (DTD). When future revenue corresponding to these amounts is realized, it will not be taxed. So there will be a corresponding future tax saving. Equation 2-1:

DTD = future non-taxable amounts.

But we also have [Equation 2-2]:

DTD = CA − TB .

From equations 2-1 and 2-2 we derive:

TB = CA − future non-taxable amounts.

To fit this formula in the schema we have been following, we can (following Picker et al, Applying IFRS standards) write the future non-taxable amount as a negative number in the FTA column.

Excluded differences

In calculating deferred tax, there are certain items that the standard requires us to exclude from the computation. These include inter alia:

  1. Goodwill (we will leave discussion of this to the advanced course).
  2. Assets (or some portion of assets) where the acquisition cost is never tax deductible (unless they are acquired in a takeover—we will leave that situation to the advanced course).

The book-tax differences for the non-deductible asset are really more like permanent than temporary differences (except that they will mechanically become zero when the asset life ends).

Example 9: Industrial buildings

In a particular country, industrial buildings are not eligible for any allowances (i.e., no tax depreciation or no tax deductions at any point). Moreover, they are not eligible for capital gains tax when sold. We exclude the book-tax differences arising from such buildings in our deferred tax calculations.

Deferred tax worksheet

At year-end, we can estimate deferred tax liability and asset balances by preparing a deferred tax worksheet.

In the worksheet, we list assets and liabilities, and use the analytical procedure illustrated above to compute the related taxable temporary differences or deductible temporary differences.

We take the sum of the taxable temporary differences. We multiply this by the income tax rate to get the deferred tax liability. (We may have multiple tax rates, e.g., income tax rate and capital gains tax rate, in which case we sum up the TTDs for which each tax rate applies, and multiply that subtotal by that tax rate.)

We take the sum of the deductible temporary differences. We multiply this by the income tax rate to get the deferred tax asset.

We will follow the practice of first computing TTDs or DTDs for excluded items, and then explicitly subtracting them from the total. This provides a record that we have tracked these items and dealt with them correctly.

Having calculated the deferred tax liability and asset, we compare them with the beginning balances for the year. This enables us to work out the required movements in each. At this point, we factor in any part of the movement that has already been recorded during the year. For example, when PPE is revalued under the revaluation model, the movement in deferred tax liability from the revaluation gain is recorded at the point of revaluation.

Balance-sheet presentation

Noncurrent items

Under IFRS, deferred tax assets and liabilities are always noncurrent.

Netting

Deferred tax assets and liabilities must be shown as separate items. The exception is when the entity is legally entitled to offset them against each other, in which case, they must be shown as a single item—net deferred tax liability, or net deferred tax asset.
