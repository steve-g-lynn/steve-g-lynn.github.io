Permanent and temporary differences

In calculating deferred tax, we distinguish between permanent and temporary book-tax differences. In calculating deferred tax, we only consider temporary differences.

Temporary differences between book and tax accounts are differences that arise from balance-sheet items where over the life of the item, the accumulated differences over the years will be zero. That is the current difference will be matched by an opposite difference some time in the future, or will reverse in the future.

One temporary difference is the difference between book amortization and tax allowances on the IP asset. Over the life of the IP asset, the cumulative book amortization must equal the cumulative tax allowances so that the lower-than-tax book amortization in the current year must be offset by a higher-than-tax amortization in future (in Year 6 of the IP asset life, when the tax allowance is 0).

Another temporary difference arises from the environmental provision. The provision expense anticipates a future claim (in 2026) that will lead to a tax deduction. Thus, we expect that the current higher book expense will be offset by a matching higher tax deduction in 2026.

Permanent differences between book and tax accounts are differences that do not mechanically reverse over time. We treat these as true economic differences rather than arising purely from different accounting; and we make no attempt to adjust for them using deferred tax.

One permanent difference is the difference arising from dividend income which is part of (book) PBT but not part of (tax) taxable profit. This is a true tax-free income that will not be offset in future by higher taxes.

Another permanent difference arises from the political contribution expense, which reduces (book) PBT, but does not reduce taxable profit. This is a true extra tax burden, which will not be offset in future by any tax relief.

The Liability approach

In computing temporary book-tax differences, rather than comparing income statement items, we compare financial-reporting balance-sheet items with their counterparts on a hypothetical balance sheet prepared using tax rules rather than financial reporting rules. The tax basis of a balance-sheet item is its value on this tax-based balance sheet.

This general approach to accounting for deferred tax is called the asset-liability approach or for short the liability approach.

Calculation for an asset (CA > TB)

If an asset has a higher (book) carrying amount (CA say) than its tax basis (TB say), then the difference (which will reverse over time) corresponds to future higher tax obligations. We recognize this as a liability called deferred tax liability (DTL). The differences are called taxable temporary differences (TTD). We compute the DTL by multiplying the TTD by the best estimate of the future income tax rate (taxrate).

TTD = CA - TB . DTL = TTD × taxrate = ( CA - TB ) × taxrate .

The logic can be explained as follows: for an asset, its carrying amount on the books represents a stored value--an estimate of the future benefits that the asset will produce. When these future benefits occur, they will lead to future taxable income, say "future taxable amounts" (FTA). But this taxable income will be offset by any related deductions, such as the remaining allowances in the case of the IP asset in our example (which equal the TB in the IP asset case), say " future deductible amounts" (FDA). The net amount of future taxable amount (FTA minus FDA) is the TTD, which matches CA minus TB for our IP asset example (also more generally, we will discuss that in another lesson).

Calculation for an asset (CA < TB)

If an asset has a lower (book) carrying amount (CA say) than its tax basis (TB say), then the difference corresponds to future tax savings (or realizes tax payments anticipated earlier). We recognize this as an asset called deferred tax asset (DTA) (or a reduction in a DTL). The differences are called deductible temporary differences (DTD). We compute the DTA by multiplying the DTD by the best estimate of the future income tax rate (taxrate).

DTD = TB - CA . DTA = DTD × taxrate = ( TB - CA ) × taxrate .

The logic can be explained as follows: for an asset, its carrying amount on the books represents a stored value--an estimate of the future benefits that the asset will produce. When these future benefits occur, they will lead to future taxable income, say "future taxable amounts" (FTA). But this taxable income will be offset by any related deductions, such as the remaining allowances in the case of the IP asset in our example (which equal the TB in the IP asset case), say " future deductible amounts" (FDA). If the future deductions are higher than the future taxable amount, then we expect tax savings. The net amount of future deductions (FDA minus FTA, equal to CA minus TB) is the basis for the future tax savings that we record via a deferred tax asset.

Calculation for a liability (CA > TB)

If a liability has a higher (book) carrying amount (CA say) than its tax basis (TB say), then the difference (which will reverse over time) corresponds to future higher tax deductions, or future tax savings. We recognize this as an asset called deferred tax asset (DTA). The differences are called deductible temporary differences (DTD). We compute the DTA by multiplying the DTD by the best estimate of the future income tax rate (taxrate).

DTD = CA - TB . DTA = DTD × taxrate = ( CA - TB ) × taxrate .

Calculation for a liability (CA < TB)

If a liability has a lower (book) carrying amount (CA say) than its tax basis (TB say), then the difference corresponds to future higher tax obligations (or a realization of tax savings anticipated earlier). We recognize this as a liability called deferred tax liability (DTL) (or a reduction in a DTA). The differences are called taxable temporary differences (TTD). We compute the DTL by multiplying the TTD by the best estimate of the future income tax rate (taxrate).

TTD = TB - CA . DTL = TTD × taxrate = ( TB - CA ) × taxrate .

Deferred tax liabilities and deferred tax assets are always noncurrent (in IFRS).

In computing deferred tax liabilities and deferred tax assets, we never apply discounting, that is, we do not calculate a present value.

Tax loss carryforwards

If a company has a loss in its tax computation, the tax rules may permit carrying the loss forward to offset against taxable profits in a future year.

In such a situation, the company computes a deferred tax asset as:

Tax Loss × Tax Rate

This deferred tax asset should only be recognized if the company can reasonably expect to realize the related tax savings in future. This means that the DTA should only be recognized to the extent that the company expects to earn sufficient future profits to cover the current tax loss. If a potential deferred tax asset cannot be recognized, the related amounts must still be disclosed in the notes.

Operating loss carryback

The tax rules may also permit carrying a tax loss back to offset taxable profits in the previous year and thereby to reduce the income tax burden from the previous year.

If a company elects to carry back its tax loss, it will receive a refund from the previous year's tax burden. It can record this as a receivable.
